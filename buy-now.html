What to Avoid

Overvalued or high risk
  • Patek Nautilus Tiffany Blue (5711/1A-018)
    $1.68M for a $52K retail watch. Pure speculation at a price point where liquidity evaporates. Only 170 exist — finding a buyer at exit is the problem.
  • F.P. Journe (at current levels)
    Auction results hit 176% of high estimates in 2025. The appreciation story is real but may be priced in. Analysts flag cooling risk. Wait for a pullback.
  • Rolex Daytona Panda (126500LN, white)
    $32K–$40K is historically elevated. Vulnerable to any macro downturn. The premium is real but the entry point isn't favorable right now.
  • Any mid-tier brand at retail
    Outside of Rolex, Patek, and AP, the average watch trades at −31% vs. retail. Omega, TAG, Breitling, Longines — buy these pre-owned or not at all as investments.
  • Gold or precious metal dress watches
    Steel sports models outperform gold/platinum consistently. Precious metal pieces from Rolex (Day-Date, gold Subs) depreciate 10–20% initially and rarely recover.

Buying Rules

1. Buy at retail whenever possible. The entire Rolex investment thesis depends on buying at MSRP from an AD. At secondary market prices, your upside is compressed and you're absorbing someone else's premium.
2. Full set or walk away. Box, papers, warranty card, hang tags. Complete sets add 15–40% to resale value. Buying without papers is buying a depreciation trap.
3. Never polish or modify. Original, unpolished cases with untouched dials hold maximum value. A single aftermarket modification can cut value by 50%.
4. Budget for carrying costs. Insurance ($500–$5K/yr) + servicing ($800–$5K every 5yr) + transaction costs (11–30% round-trip). A watch appreciating 5%/yr but costing 3%/yr to hold is a 2% asset.
5. Hold for 10+ years. Transaction costs alone wipe out ~3 years of appreciation. Short-term flipping is a losing game after the 2022 correction reset margins.
