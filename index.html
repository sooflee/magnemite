What predicts luxury watch investment returns?

~12 min read · compiled May 2026 · data from WatchCharts, Bloomberg Subdial50, Knight Frank KFLII, Bob's Watches, SSRN

Executive summary

  • Factor #1: Brand. Only three brands — Rolex, Patek Philippe, Audemars Piguet — have positive average value retention in the secondary market. Excluding those three, the average watch trades at −31% vs. retail.
  • Factor #2: Discontinuation. Frozen supply is the single strongest short-term catalyst. The Rolex Hulk, Patek 5711, and (as of April 2026) Rolex Pepsi GMT all surged after discontinuation.
  • Factor #3: Material paradox. Stainless steel sports watches command 20–90%+ premiums over retail. Precious metal dress watches depreciate 10–20% immediately. The cheapest material appreciates the most.
  • Market timing. The WatchCharts Overall Index is +8.2% over the past year after a 13-quarter correction. Prices are accessible relative to 2022 peaks but remain above pre-pandemic levels.
  • Non-obvious finding. Transaction costs (11–30% round-trip) are the dominant drag on net returns — a single buy-sell cycle wipes out ~3 years of 5% annual appreciation.

How to read this study

Value retention
The ratio of secondary-market price to original MSRP, expressed as a percentage. +20% means the watch trades at 120% of retail. Data from WatchCharts brand indices.
Premium / discount to retail
Current secondary market price minus current MSRP. Positive = trades above retail (demand exceeds supply). Negative = depreciation.
CAGR
Compound annual growth rate — the smoothed annualized return over a period. We report both nominal CAGR and inflation-adjusted where noted.
Factor weight
A normalized importance score (0–1) indicating how strongly a quality predicts appreciation, derived from secondary market data across 50+ actively traded references. Higher = more predictive.
Signal (Buy / Hold / Caution / Avoid)
An editorial assessment combining current valuation relative to historical range, factor scores, and macro timing. Not a recommendation — see caveats.

1. What predicts watch appreciation?

Bottom line: Six observable factors explain most of the cross-sectional variation in watch value retention. Brand and discontinuation status dominate; dial color and cultural significance provide secondary signal. Production volume and waitlist length are proxies for the underlying scarcity that drives all of it.

We examine every factor that varies across watch references and ask: does this quality predict whether a watch will trade above or below retail on the secondary market? The table below ranks factors by observed predictive strength, derived from value-retention data across 50+ actively traded references from WatchCharts, Bloomberg Subdial50, and dealer sources.

2. Factor deep-dive

Bottom line: The top three factors (brand, discontinuation, material) account for most of the signal. A discontinued steel sports watch from a top-3 brand is the highest-probability appreciation play in the market. Everything else is secondary.

2b. The steel paradox

The cheapest case material consistently commands the highest secondary market premiums. Within every major collection, the steel variant outperforms gold and platinum:

Mechanism: Demand is broadest at steel price points (more buyers), while brands restrict steel allocation more aggressively than precious metal to protect waitlists.

5. Market cycle & timing

Bottom line: The market bottomed in late 2024 after a 13-quarter correction. 2025 was the first positive year since 2021. Current positioning: early recovery. Speculative froth has cleared; value-driven buyers dominate.

Key indices as of early 2026:

  • WatchCharts Overall Market Index: +8.2% year-over-year
  • Bloomberg Subdial50: +8% in 2025 (first positive year since 2021)
  • Knight Frank KFLII (watches): +125.1% over 10 years; +52.7% over 5 years
  • Global luxury watch market: $62.35B (2026), projected 8.5% CAGR to 2034
The inflation caveat. The WatchCharts index is up ~20% over five years, but US CPI exceeded 25% in the same period. In real (inflation-adjusted) terms, most watches are cheaper today than five years ago. Watches are a diversifier, not an inflation hedge. Academic evidence (Weisskopf & Masset, SSRN) confirms low correlation with stocks, bonds, and gold — diversification value is real, but expected returns trail equities (~5.7% nominal vs. 9.6–12.9% for the S&P 500).

6. Hidden costs & net returns

Bottom line: Carrying costs consume 2–3% of a watch's value annually. Transaction costs of 11–30% on a round trip mean you need to hold 5+ years just to break even against the bid-ask spread.

Net return math: A watch appreciating 5% nominally, costing 2% annually to hold, and 15% to sell over a 5-year hold delivers approximately 0% net real return after inflation. The investment case only works for references appreciating at 8%+ CAGR — which limits the viable universe to a handful of top-3-brand steel sports models.

7. Buying rules

Bottom line: Five rules that, if followed, eliminate most of the ways watch "investors" lose money.
  1. Buy at retail from an AD whenever possible. The entire Rolex investment thesis depends on the MSRP-to-secondary spread. At grey market prices, your upside is compressed.
  2. Full set or walk away. Box, papers, warranty card, hang tags. Incomplete sets are depreciation traps — they trade at a 15–40% discount to full sets.
  3. Never polish, modify, or replace parts. Original unpolished cases with untouched dials hold maximum value. A single aftermarket modification can cut value by 50%.
  4. Budget 2–3% annually for carrying costs. If you aren't budgeting for insurance and servicing, you aren't calculating your real return.
  5. Hold for 10+ years. Transaction costs wipe out ~3 years of appreciation. Short-term flipping is a losing game in the post-2022 market.

Caveats

This is analysis, not investment advice.
  • Survivorship bias. The data overweights winners. For every Daytona that appreciated 358%, dozens of watches from other brands lost 30%+ and stopped being tracked.
  • No predictive model was trained. Factor weights are inferred from observed correlations in secondary market data, not from a machine-learning model with out-of-sample validation. The ranking is directional, not calibrated.
  • Liquidity risk is real. You cannot sell a $100K watch in seconds like a stock. During market stress (2022–2024), bid-ask spreads widened dramatically.
  • Condition is everything. Our analysis assumes full-set, excellent-condition examples. Reality introduces variance that no factor model captures.
  • Macro dependency. Watch prices are correlated with disposable income, interest rates, and risk appetite. A recession or rate shock could trigger another correction regardless of reference-level factors.
  • Past performance. The 2018–2023 Rolex/Patek run may not repeat. Mean reversion is a possibility.
