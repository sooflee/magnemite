Watches that beat retail: scarcity, allocation, and the secondary market

Most watches lose money. The ones that don't share a single trait — supply allocated below demand, by lottery, drop, or waitlist — and the premium belongs to whoever gets in at retail.

~8 min read · compiled June 2026 · data from WatchCharts, Chrono24, StockX, and auction records (Phillips, Christie's, Sotheby's, Loupe This)

Text size

Summary

  • Most watches depreciate. Outside Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Audemars Piguet, the average watch trades roughly 31% below retail.
  • The watches that hold value share one trait: supply is allocated below demand — by a waitlist at the top of the market, or by a lottery, raffle, or limited drop among independents. Either way, the gain is the retail-to-resale gap, captured only by buying at retail.
  • This allocation route is the one accessible at normal prices: a handful of sub-$10,000 pieces resell at roughly 1.3–4.4× retail. The same mechanism runs up to six figures, but liquidity thins sharply, and most micro-brands resell below retail.
  • Transaction costs (~11–30% round-trip) and thin liquidity offset much of the gain. Watches are best understood as durable goods that may hold value, not as investments.

1. What predicts value retention

In short: a few observable traits explain most of whether a watch holds value. Brand and scarcity dominate; the rest are secondary.

The same question applies to any reference: will it trade above or below retail later? The factors below, ordered by how strongly they predict the outcome, account for most of the variation.

FactorDirectionEffect
BrandTop three > all others+15% to +32% retention (top three) vs. −31% (rest)
Scarcity / discontinuationFrozen or limited supply > in productionLarge, durable premiums once supply stops
Case materialSteel > precious metalSteel sports models hold value; gold dress watches drop 10–20% at once
CategorySports > dressDivers and chronographs see the broadest demand
Waitlist lengthLonger > shorterIf retail is effectively unavailable, resale rises to clear demand
Condition & completenessOriginal, full set > modifiedBox and papers add value; polishing or modification can remove it

The common thread is supply held below demand — whether by a waitlist, a discontinuation, or a small production run. Everything else is a variation on that.

2. Lotteries, raffles, and drops

In short: the most direct — and most accessible — way to own a watch that resells above retail is to win a lottery, raffle, or ballot, or to catch a timed drop before it sells out. Whoever is allocated one at retail can resell above it; most watches outside this mechanism do not appreciate.

A maker produces far fewer watches than people want and allocates the shortfall by a draw or a release that sells out in minutes. 3. Verified above retail — by waitlist and relationship

In short: the same idea at the high end, but allocated by a multi-year order book or a dealer relationship rather than a draw — independent watchmakers plus a few maison limited editions and shaped Cartiers. These resell above their original price on verified sales, though liquidity is thin and the largest multiples are single auction records, not repeatable levels.

Here a sought-after maker takes orders years ahead or places pieces with known collectors; there is no public ballot to enter. Realized prices below come almost entirely from auction, so a single result can set the figure. Treat the biggest multiples (Dufour, Rexhepi, the gold Voutilainens) as records, and the whole tier as illiquid — realizing a gain can take years and the right venue.

4. The established brands

In short: the same allocation dynamic operates at the top of the market. Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Audemars Piguet retain value in steel sports models, but only buyers allocated one at retail capture the premium — access, not capital, is the constraint.

The mechanism is the lottery mechanism in a different guise. These brands restrict supply at the retail level much as the lottery makers do — here through waitlists and purchase history rather than a draw — so the resale price sits well above retail. The return is the retail-to-resale gap, captured only by being allocated a watch at the store price.

At entry prices, value retention without the access advantage is modest: a standard Omega Speedmaster Moonwatch trades somewhat below retail pre-owned (its limited editions, like the Snoopy above, are the exception), and a Tudor Black Bay holds roughly 85–95% of retail. These are reasonable watches to own, but not appreciation stories.

5. What to treat with caution

In short: most affordable brands trade at or below retail, and online "multiples" are frequently asking prices rather than completed sales.

Below retail. The majority of accessible brands do not hold a premium. Tudor, Grand Seiko, Ressence, Czapek, and most affordable dive-watch micro-brands (Lorier, Serica, Nodus) typically resell for roughly 60–95% of retail. They are worth owning to wear, not to resell.

Asking vs. realized. Many widely repeated resale figures come from listings (a hoped-for price) rather than transactions. Several commonly cited examples did not hold up against completed-sale data:

  • A reported ~10× for Furlan Marri is not supported by realized sales; the premium is modest.
  • A "~4.5×" Autodromo Monoposto traces to a single atypical auction lot; ordinary examples sell below retail (~$375–$500).
  • "~2×" figures for various MoonSwatch, Halios, and Unimatic models are asking prices; completed sales sit near retail.
  • Studio Underd0g's "Watermel0n," sometimes cited as a ~1.7× flip, is in stock at retail (£550 / ~$750) on the brand's own site — a premium you can simply decline to pay. Only sold-out earlier editions trade meaningfully above retail.

A practical filter: rely on prices someone actually paid — completed auction results or platform last-sale data — not on active listings.

6. Costs & net returns

In short: selling costs and limited liquidity absorb much of any gain, so the net case is narrower than headline premiums suggest.

Realizing a gain involves selling, and selling is expensive. A round trip typically costs 11–30% once dealer spreads or platform fees are included, and finding a buyer can take weeks or months — watches are not liquid like listed securities. Ongoing insurance and servicing add roughly 2–3% per year. A watch appreciating 5% annually but costing 3% to hold returns little after costs and inflation.

The cases that clear these costs are narrow: established references bought at retail, and the lottery- or drop-allocated independents above when acquired at retail. Most other watches, at best, break even.

7. Buying principles

  1. The premium is in the retail-to-resale gap. Buying at retail captures it; buying on the secondary market means paying it.
  2. Use realized prices. Judge value from completed sales, not from active listings.
  3. Scarcity is more durable than attention. A lottery or a capped run sustains a premium; general hype fades.
  4. For release-driven pieces, time matters. Any premium is usually largest in the first weeks after a drop.
  5. Keep it original and complete. Box and papers preserve value; polishing or modification can reduce it.

Caveats

This is informational, not financial advice.
  • Survivorship bias. Coverage skews toward watches that held value; many comparable references quietly depreciated and stopped being tracked.
  • Prices move. Figures reflect 2024–2026 conditions and shift with the market and exchange rates.
  • Liquidity is limited. Selling can be slow, and spreads widen during downturns.
  • Condition varies. Quoted figures assume original, full-set, excellent-condition examples.
