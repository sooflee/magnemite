Do watches hold their value?

~8 min read · compiled June 2026 · data from WatchCharts, Chrono24, StockX, and auction records (Phillips, Christie's, Sotheby's, Loupe This)

Summary

  • Most watches depreciate. Outside Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Audemars Piguet, the average watch trades roughly 31% below retail on the secondary market.
  • Those three brands retain value primarily in their steel sports models. The gain is largely the gap between retail and resale — so it depends on being able to buy at retail, which is the binding constraint.
  • A narrower exception exists at lower prices: some independents and micro-brands release watches by lottery or limited drop that resell above retail shortly after. A handful roughly double; most do not.
  • Transaction costs (~11–30% round-trip) and thin liquidity offset much of the gain. Watches are best understood as durable goods that may hold value, not as investments.

1. What predicts value retention

In short: a few observable traits explain most of whether a watch holds value. Brand and scarcity dominate; the rest are secondary.

The same question applies to any reference: will it trade above or below retail later? The factors below, ordered by how strongly they predict the outcome, account for most of the variation.

The common thread is supply held below demand — whether by a waitlist, a discontinuation, or a small production run. Everything else is a variation on that.

2. The established segment

In short: Rolex, Patek Philippe, and Audemars Piguet retain value in steel sports models. The premium is realized only if the watch is bought at retail; access, not capital, is the limiting factor.

For these references the resale price sits well above retail, but the brands restrict supply at the retail level. The return is the retail-to-resale gap, which a buyer captures only by being allocated a watch at the store price — often a matter of waitlists and purchase history.

At entry prices, value retention without the access advantage is modest: an Omega Speedmaster trades somewhat below retail pre-owned, and a Tudor Black Bay holds roughly 85–95% of retail. These are reasonable watches to own, but not appreciation stories.

3. The accessible segment

In short: some independents and micro-brands apply the same scarcity mechanism at far lower prices — small runs, timed drops, or lotteries — and the resulting pieces can resell above retail. The premium is captured only by acquiring at retail.

The mechanism is identical to the established segment: production is set well below demand, here by a lottery, a single timed release, or a limited edition. The figures below reflect realized resale prices rather than asking prices, and reflect 2024–2026 data.

As in the established segment, the premium belongs to whoever buys at retail. Purchasing on the secondary market means paying the premium rather than earning it. For pieces driven by a release event, any premium tends to be largest soon after the drop and to compress over time.

At much higher prices, a few independents (for example Roger W. Smith, Vianney Halter, Atelier de Chronométrie) also resell well above their original prices, but these are six-figure, auction-dependent watches with limited liquidity — a different segment from the accessible pieces above.

4. What to treat with caution

In short: most affordable brands trade at or below retail, and online "multiples" are frequently asking prices rather than completed sales.

Below retail. The majority of accessible brands do not hold a premium. Tudor, Grand Seiko, Ressence, Czapek, and most affordable dive-watch micro-brands (Lorier, Serica, Nodus) typically resell for roughly 60–95% of retail. They are worth owning to wear, not to resell.

Asking vs. realized. Many widely repeated resale figures come from listings (a hoped-for price) rather than transactions. Several commonly cited examples did not hold up against completed-sale data:

  • A reported ~10× for Furlan Marri is not supported by realized sales; the premium is modest.
  • A "~4.5×" Autodromo Monoposto traces to a single atypical auction lot; ordinary examples sell below retail (~$375–$500).
  • "~2×" figures for various MoonSwatch, Halios, and Unimatic models are asking prices; completed sales sit near retail.

A practical filter: rely on prices someone actually paid — completed auction results or platform last-sale data — not on active listings.

5. Costs & net returns

In short: selling costs and limited liquidity absorb much of any gain, so the net case is narrower than headline premiums suggest.

Realizing a gain involves selling, and selling is expensive. A round trip typically costs 11–30% once dealer spreads or platform fees are included, and finding a buyer can take weeks or months — watches are not liquid like listed securities. Ongoing insurance and servicing add roughly 2–3% per year. A watch appreciating 5% annually but costing 3% to hold returns little after costs and inflation.

The cases that clear these costs are narrow: established references bought at retail, and the lottery- or drop-allocated independents above when acquired at retail. Most other watches, at best, break even.

6. Buying principles

  1. The premium is in the retail-to-resale gap. Buying at retail captures it; buying on the secondary market means paying it.
  2. Use realized prices. Judge value from completed sales, not from active listings.
  3. Scarcity is more durable than attention. A lottery or a capped run sustains a premium; general hype fades.
  4. For release-driven pieces, time matters. Any premium is usually largest in the first weeks after a drop.
  5. Keep it original and complete. Box and papers preserve value; polishing or modification can reduce it.

Caveats

This is informational, not financial advice.
  • Survivorship bias. Coverage skews toward watches that held value; many comparable references quietly depreciated and stopped being tracked.
  • Prices move. Figures reflect 2024–2026 conditions and shift with the market and exchange rates.
  • Liquidity is limited. Selling can be slow, and spreads widen during downturns.
  • Condition varies. Quoted figures assume original, full-set, excellent-condition examples.
